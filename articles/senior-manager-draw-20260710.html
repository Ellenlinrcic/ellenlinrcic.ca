高级管理人员类别分数降至392分：加拿大正在优先保留哪些人才？

加拿大移民部于2026年7月10日进行新一轮 Express Entry 快速通道抽签。本轮抽签针对具备加拿大工作经验的高级管理人员类别，共发出500份永久居民申请邀请，最低综合排名系统分数为392分。

这是该类别设立以来的第二次专项抽签。

第一次抽签于2026年3月5日进行，共发出250份邀请，最低分数为429分。与首次抽签相比，本轮邀请人数增加一倍，最低分数则下降37分。

抽签日期 邀请人数 最低 CRS 分数
2026年3月5日 250人 429分
2026年7月10日 500人 392分

虽然目前只有两次抽签，尚不足以判断这一类别未来会维持怎样的固定分数，但邀请人数增加、分数明显下降，已经释放出一个相当清晰的政策信号：

加拿大希望通过 Express Entry，保留已经进入加拿大劳动力市场，并且承担企业或机构高级决策职责的人才。

一、这不是普通的管理人员类别

高级管理人员类别是加拿大2026年新增的 Express Entry 类别之一。

这一类别并不适用于所有职位名称中带有“Manager”的申请人。其范围集中在 NOC 00012至00015四个高级管理职业，覆盖金融、商业服务、医疗、教育、社会服务、贸易、建筑、运输、生产和公用事业等领域的高级管理人员。

与一般部门经理、门店经理或者项目经理相比，这类人员通常处于企业或机构较高决策层，其职责更多涉及：

  • 制定整体发展战略；
  • 决定企业或机构的重要政策；
  • 配置人力和财务资源；
  • 监督主要部门或其他管理人员；
  • 对整体经营方向和结果承担责任。

因此，这一类别关注的并不是一个人的职位名称是否听起来“高级”，而是其在企业或机构中是否真正承担战略决策和整体管理职能。

二、392分的真正意义，不只是“分数低”

本轮392分的最低邀请分数，明显低于近期普通 Canadian Experience Class 抽签所需要的分数。

它所体现的政策意义，是加拿大正在进一步弱化“所有人统一按照 CRS 总分竞争”的模式。

在类别抽签制度下，不同候选人实际上进入了不同的竞争范围。一个 CRS 只有392分、但符合高级管理人员类别的申请人，可能获得邀请；而一个 CRS 超过500分、但不符合当期项目或者类别要求的申请人，仍然可能继续留在候选池中。

这说明 Express Entry 的筛选逻辑正在发生变化：CRS 分数仍然重要，但申请人是否属于加拿大当前优先保留的人才类别，正在变得同样重要，甚至在某些情况下更加重要。

三、这一类别缓解了高级人才与年龄评分之间的矛盾

高级管理职位通常需要较长时间的职业积累。

很多人需要经过多年工作，从专业岗位、部门主管、中层管理逐步进入企业决策层。等到真正具备高级管理经验时，年龄往往已经超过35岁，甚至达到40岁以上。

但 Express Entry 评分体系在年龄方面明显偏向年轻申请人。申请人从30岁以后开始逐年失去年龄分数，45岁以后年龄得分降为零。

这形成了一个长期存在的矛盾：加拿大希望吸引和保留经验丰富、能够承担企业决策职责的人才，但传统 CRS 评分体系却可能因为年龄因素，使这些人的总分缺乏竞争力。

高级管理人员类别在一定程度上修正了这一矛盾。

本轮分数降至392分，意味着加拿大不再要求这类申请人必须在普通 CEC 抽签中，与年龄更小、学历和语言分数更高的候选人直接竞争。

从政策角度看，这相当于承认：对于某些需要长期职业积累的职位，经验、决策能力和对加拿大经济的潜在贡献，不能完全由年龄和语言分数组成的 CRS 体系来衡量。

四、加拿大正在优先保留已经证明本地价值的人

这个类别最重要的限制之一，是相关高级管理经验必须在加拿大取得。这一要求非常关键。

它说明加拿大此次并不是面向全球广泛吸引所有高级管理人员，而是优先考虑已经来到加拿大、已经参与本地经济活动，并且已经在加拿大企业或机构中担任重要职务的人。

这与近年来加拿大移民政策中不断加强的“境内优先”方向一致。

对于政府而言，这类申请人已经：

  • 在加拿大工作；
  • 参与本地企业或机构运营；
  • 对加拿大商业环境有一定了解；
  • 已经与本地劳动力市场建立联系；
  • 相比完全没有加拿大经历的海外申请人，融入风险更低。

因此，这个类别并不仅仅是一个“职业类别”，也是加拿大境内人才保留政策的一部分。

五、哪些人最可能从中受益？

  1. 年龄较大但经验丰富的高级管理人员；
  2. 跨国公司内部调派到加拿大的高管；
  3. 加拿大企业中的总裁、副总裁和高级负责人；
  4. 在加拿大完成职业晋升的人。

六、对普通 Manager 意味着什么？

这一类别对高级管理人员非常有利，但并不意味着所有管理人员都能受益。

Restaurant Manager、Retail Manager、Office Manager、Marketing Manager、Financial Manager、Construction Manager 等职位，虽然可能属于管理类职业，但并不当然属于 NOC 00012至00015的高级管理人员范围。

这也说明加拿大此次筛选的目标非常明确：它不是广泛降低所有管理人员的移民门槛，而是集中保留处于企业或机构较高决策层的人。

七、Express Entry 正在从“统一高分竞争”转向“分类筛选”

高级管理人员类别与法语、医疗、技工、研究人员等类别一起，反映出 Express Entry 正在不断强化分类筛选。

过去，申请人更关注的是：“我的 CRS 是否足够高？”

现在，越来越重要的问题变成：“我的职业是否符合加拿大当前优先选择的类别？”

这意味着 Express Entry 正在从一个相对单一的积分排名系统，逐渐演变为一个兼具积分和政策导向的筛选系统。

CRS 决定申请人在特定类别中的排名，但类别本身决定申请人是否能够进入当期的竞争范围。

对申请人而言，这种变化可能带来更大的机会差异。同样的 CRS 分数，因为职业、语言能力和加拿大工作经验不同，获邀概率可能完全不同。

八、对加拿大移民政策意味着什么？

高级管理人员类别的出现，也说明加拿大正在尝试更加精细地定义“经济类移民价值”。

传统 Express Entry 评分体系主要看年龄、学历、语言和工作经验，这些因素相对容易量化，但不一定能够充分反映一个申请人的职业影响力。

高级管理人员的价值往往体现在：

  • 战略决策能力；
  • 企业管理经验；
  • 投资和资源配置能力；
  • 带动就业和业务发展的能力；
  • 对特定行业或机构的整体影响。

这些能力很难完全通过传统 CRS 评分体现。

因此，设立专项类别，本质上是政府在传统积分制度之外，加入更明确的经济政策判断。

这也说明未来 Express Entry 可能继续围绕加拿大经济和劳动力市场需要，进行更加细分的人才筛选。

结语

高级管理人员类别第二次抽签，邀请人数从250人增加到500人，最低分数从429分降至392分。这一变化对真正具备加拿大高级管理经验的人来说，是一个非常积极的信号。

特别是那些因年龄因素在普通 CEC 抽签中失去竞争力，但已经在加拿大承担企业战略、资源配置和高级决策职责的人，可能成为这一政策最直接的受益者。

但这次抽签最重要的意义，并不只是出现了一个392分的低分数。

它说明了一个趋势：加拿大正在通过 Express Entry，将有限的永久居民名额，更有针对性地分配给已经在加拿大证明自身价值、并符合当前经济政策方向的人才。

对于未来的 Express Entry 申请人来说，竞争的关键将不再只是“总分有多高”，还包括“自己是否属于加拿大想优先保留的人”。

Senior Manager Category CRS Drops to 392: What Kind of Talent Is Canada Prioritizing?

On July 10, 2026, Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada conducted a new Express Entry draw targeting senior managers with Canadian work experience. A total of 500 Invitations to Apply for permanent residence were issued, with a minimum Comprehensive Ranking System score of 392.

This was the second category-based draw held for senior managers since the category was introduced.

The first draw took place on March 5, 2026, when 250 invitations were issued at a minimum CRS score of 429. Compared with the first draw, the number of invitations doubled, while the minimum score fell by 37 points.

Draw date Invitations issued Minimum CRS score
March 5, 2026 250 429
July 10, 2026 500 392

Only two draws have taken place so far, so it is still too early to predict where the score for this category may stabilize.

However, the increase in invitations and the significant drop in the cut-off score send a clear policy signal: Canada is using Express Entry to retain individuals who have already entered the Canadian labour market and who hold senior decision-making responsibilities.

1. This Is Not a Category for All Managers

The senior manager category is one of the new Express Entry categories introduced by Canada in 2026.

It does not apply to every applicant whose job title includes the word “Manager.” The category is limited to four senior management occupations under NOC codes 00012 to 00015.

These occupations cover senior managers in financial and business services, health and education, social and community services, trade, construction, transportation, production and utilities.

Compared with department managers, store managers or project managers, individuals in these occupations generally operate at a higher decision-making level within a business or institution.

Their responsibilities are more likely to involve:

  • establishing overall organizational strategy;
  • determining major corporate or institutional policies;
  • allocating human and financial resources;
  • supervising major departments or other managers; and
  • assuming responsibility for the organization’s overall direction and performance.

The category therefore focuses not on whether a job title sounds senior, but on whether the individual genuinely performs strategic and organization-wide management functions.

2. The Real Significance of a 392 CRS Score

The minimum score of 392 is substantially lower than the scores typically required in recent Canadian Experience Class draws.

The policy significance is not simply that the score is low. It reflects Canada’s continued movement away from a system in which all candidates compete only through one overall CRS ranking.

Under category-based selection, different groups of candidates are effectively competing within different pools.

A candidate with a CRS score of 392 who qualifies under the senior manager category may receive an invitation, while another candidate with a CRS score above 500 who does not qualify under the program or category selected in that round may remain in the Express Entry pool.

CRS scores continue to matter, but whether an applicant belongs to a category that Canada currently wishes to prioritize is becoming equally important and, in some cases, even more important.

3. Addressing the Tension Between Seniority and Age-Based Scoring

Senior management positions usually require many years of career development.

Many individuals progress from professional roles to supervisory positions, then to middle management, before eventually reaching the executive or senior decision-making level.

By the time they acquire genuine senior management experience, they may already be over 35 or even over 40.

The Express Entry scoring system, however, strongly favours younger candidates. Applicants begin losing age points after age 29, and applicants aged 45 or older receive no CRS points for age.

This creates a longstanding tension. Canada may wish to attract and retain experienced individuals capable of making high-level business decisions, but the traditional CRS system may leave those same candidates at a disadvantage because of their age.

The senior manager category partially corrects this imbalance.

A cut-off score of 392 means that eligible senior managers are no longer required to compete directly in regular CEC draws against younger candidates with stronger age, education and language scores.

For occupations requiring long-term career development, experience, decision-making ability and potential economic contribution cannot be measured entirely through a CRS formula heavily influenced by age and language results.

4. Canada Is Prioritizing People Who Have Already Demonstrated Value in Canada

One of the most important restrictions under this category is that the qualifying senior management experience must have been gained in Canada.

This shows that Canada is not broadly recruiting senior managers from around the world through this category.

Instead, it is prioritizing individuals who are already in Canada, are already participating in the Canadian economy and already hold important positions within Canadian businesses or institutions.

This is consistent with the broader policy direction of prioritizing candidates already established in Canada.

From the government’s perspective, these individuals have already:

  • worked in Canada;
  • participated in the operation of Canadian businesses or institutions;
  • developed familiarity with the Canadian business environment;
  • established ties to the Canadian labour market; and
  • demonstrated a lower integration risk than applicants with no Canadian experience.

The category is therefore not merely occupation-based. It also forms part of a broader strategy to retain talent already contributing to Canada.

5. Who Is Most Likely to Benefit?

  1. Older senior managers with extensive experience;
  2. Executives transferred to Canada by multinational companies;
  3. Presidents, vice-presidents and senior corporate leaders in Canada;
  4. Individuals who advanced into senior roles in Canada.

6. What Does This Mean for Ordinary Managers?

The category is highly favourable for senior managers, but it does not benefit all individuals working in management positions.

Restaurant managers, retail managers, office managers, marketing managers, financial managers and construction managers may all work in management occupations, but they do not automatically fall within NOC codes 00012 to 00015.

The government is not broadly lowering the immigration threshold for all managers. It is concentrating on individuals who operate at a high strategic and organizational decision-making level.

7. Express Entry Is Moving from Broad Competition to Targeted Selection

The senior manager category, together with categories for French-language proficiency, health care, trades and researchers, shows that Express Entry is increasingly based on targeted selection.

In the past, applicants mainly asked: “Is my CRS score high enough?”

The more important question is now becoming: “Does my occupation or profile fall within a category Canada currently wants to prioritize?”

Express Entry is gradually evolving from a relatively straightforward points-ranking system into a system combining points with specific policy objectives.

CRS determines a candidate’s ranking within a selected group, but the category itself determines whether the candidate enters the relevant competition.

Candidates with the same CRS score may face completely different prospects depending on their occupation, language ability and type of Canadian work experience.

8. What Does This Mean for Canadian Immigration Policy?

The creation of the senior manager category shows that Canada is trying to define the value of economic immigration more precisely.

The traditional Express Entry system focuses primarily on age, education, language ability and work experience. These factors are relatively easy to measure, but they may not fully capture an applicant’s professional influence.

The value of senior managers often lies in:

  • strategic decision-making ability;
  • business leadership experience;
  • investment and resource allocation;
  • the ability to support employment and business growth; and
  • influence over the direction of a particular organization or sector.

These qualities are difficult to measure fully through a traditional CRS score.

The creation of a dedicated category therefore represents the government adding a clearer economic policy judgment to the existing points system.

It also suggests that future Express Entry selection may become increasingly specialized around Canada’s economic and labour market priorities.

Conclusion

In the second senior manager category draw, the number of invitations increased from 250 to 500, while the minimum CRS score fell from 429 to 392.

This is a highly positive development for individuals with genuine Canadian senior management experience.

The most important significance of this draw, however, is not simply the appearance of a 392 cut-off score.

It reflects a broader trend: Canada is using Express Entry to allocate a limited number of permanent residence opportunities more selectively to people who have already demonstrated their value in Canada and who align with current economic policy priorities.

For future Express Entry candidates, the key question will no longer be only, “How high is my CRS score?” It will also be: “Am I part of the talent Canada wants to retain?”
